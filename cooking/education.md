I've been thinking about education for a while now. Some time ago, listening to Remzi's lecture about operating systems, I heard him say something like this to his students: "Don't just learn the general aspect of things; learn the details. Having just a notion of a subject is precisely the definition of ignorance."

This idea from Remzi got me thinking, and today I want to write about what I've been reflecting on this matter.

### Understanding the details of things

When we don't understand the details of things, we fall into the following traps: 1. we think we know something that we don't actually know; 2. we develop preconceptions about it, which clouds our judgment; 3. we lose the chance to effectively understand something by considering that the little we know is all there is to be understood about that subject.

To go deep into a topic, however, we need time, and we need effort. We dig a hole with our bare hands to unearth a hidden treasure. One must be willing to deal with the dirty part of things -- and the details are usually dirty, and complicated. It takes consistency, and, like it or not, love for it. Understanding something deeply will always be a mission in the name of love.

On the other hand, as I said, our most important and limited resource is time; and consequently we can never understand all things in depth. This task is restricted to a small circle of meaningful interests.

### Understanding things abstractly is part of the education process

I remember when, after spending a few years studying Greek philosophy, I decided to read Kant and modern philosophy in general. My personal interest in Kant and modern philosophy was almost null. What I understood from, say, Descartes, was only a tiny portion that corresponded to what I already knew from Greek metaphysics. All his new ideas passed through me like harmless whispers.

I wasn't prepared to understand Descartes, in various ways: 1. I wasn't truly interested in his philosophy; 2. I hadn't read any books that exposed me to the concepts of modern philosophy, idealism and subjectivism. In conclusion, reading Descartes I saw only the shadow, in him, that communicated with Plato and Aristotle.

On the other hand, by reading him I exposed myself to a new type of universe, unknown to me until then. Although I didn't know what idealism really was, I knew there was something called "idealism". Although I couldn't precisely discourse about his Meditations, I knew however, in a vague and sketchy way, what was said there. And what was the advantage in that? Everything I learned there, from reading superficially, came to inhabit my inner map.

Years later, I tried again. This time, however, I read his precursors. I read Bacon, a bit of Locke. I picked up books on the history of modern philosophy. I understood what the deductive method was before reading, again, any of his works on epistemology.

### How do I study today?
A preamble: my "natural" method of studying is quite chaotic: I follow rabbit holes, fall into them, and rarely manage to get out in time. The truth is that I follow my interests in a wobbling, poorly organized way, without a final goal in mind.

![alt](/static/education/rabbit-hole.jpg)

However, I often need to study something that isn't closely tied to my particular interests. And for this, strategy is necessary.

The first tactic I usually employ is trying to instigate some personal interest in the subject, just as I did with Descartes and Kant. For this, I read a bit about the historical context of that subject; I read what other non-professionals say about it; I try to understand what kind of universe that subject conjures. After that, I get a bit closer, and I start circling the subject itself, approaching it more and more, but always with the support of other works, other opinions, always keeping my eyes on the metadata of that subject.

I approach the subject in separate cycles. For example: in the second stage -- the stage of approach itself -- is done based on a very concrete objective, such as: "I know nothing about SOLID, just its historical context; now I'm going to study these principles with the goal of creating a POC of no more than 15 lines of code for each of the principles". This is the first cycle. After it, if I need to -- because of work or something else -- I create a new objective, also tangible, that guides me and gives me the feeling of completing something.

The technique I use for topics I'm interested in is the question-answer approach. I keep a list with various questions about anything that catches my interest. For example: "what's the kernel's network stack?", "how do FreeBSD jails work?", among many others. When I have some free time, I pick one of these topics - like those claw machines grabbing stuffed bears at the mall - and try to answer them from start to finish.

![alt](/static/education/claw-machine.jpg)

In the end, it's important to be aware that study is done in concentric circles that overlap each other. For subjects more distant from us, the circles are represented by concrete objectives; for subjects closer to us, which have personal value, the circles are represented by constant iterations guided by our own voluntary interest.

### Sparking interest

I mentioned understanding circumstances and historical contexts, and reading what others think about the topic. I often read parts of books - sometimes just skimming through the table of contents is enough to get a feel for something. I love reading Reddit, forums, discussion channels, collecting opinions, general impressions, vibes. I think it's important to have a vast and rich library (even if it's virtual). Flipping through a random book might just kickstart a whole new journey.

Chatting with people who know their stuff is also something I enjoy doing. Not long ago, during one of my rabbit holes while studying namespaces and the history of containers, I talked with a friend who had worked before containers were even a thing. I learned some fascinating stuff about how developers used to work to isolate resources on a single machine.

### Keeping at it

My biggest piece of advice is to set tiny, achievable goals. And keep it real: know that you're studying this thing so something else can happen. Set minimal criteria that won't mess with what you actually want to do with your day. This has been working for me for over a decade.

Learning is a bit like cooking - sometimes you follow a recipe strictly, other times you just throw things together based on instinct. Both approaches can work, but you need to know when to use each.
