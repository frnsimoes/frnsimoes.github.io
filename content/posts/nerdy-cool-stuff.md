Being a software developer is a really hard task. We all know this. When you are a self-taught developer, it's even harder—not because you didn’t have formal education, but because you don’t even know what the best formal education is all about. You learn some Python or JavaScript and start scripting, building a thing or two, following traces of errors and fixing them, searching for solutions online. But there’s a potential prejudicial effect in learning this way: you think that software engineering is all about doing.

Well, it is. But not in the way we, as beginners with a self-taught mindset, imagine. People put an enormous amount of effort behind every tool we use. The concepts we have at hand to solve problems were discovered, debated, experimented with, and battle-tested for years. If we think, for example, about an API: there are a lot of things behind the simple act of building a list endpoint. People had to write and think about distributed inter-process communication, sockets, invent and explore new protocols, techniques to handle data, etc., etc., etc. If we can, in a month or two, build our first API endpoint, we should, after some time, also understand whats goes behind the simple act of listing an endpoint.

Maybe you are rolling your eyes at me: "Hey, I'm not going to study those "byte" things!". But let me tell you: this is the most fun part of being a software developer. The computer is a magical and beautiful thing. The first time I tried to convert a binary into an hexadecimal, I hated it. I thought it was too hard, that it was not "for me". I wanted to understand things better, though, so I kept going. I learnd some systems, then I got to explore the concepts of Operating Systems, and I'm having the most fun time of my study life since I learned how to Python em 2020. 

What chocks me, though, is that I worked as a developer for a long time without even knowing that I had the opportunity to learn these things. I kenw some Python, and Django, and I was doing my job well (enough?). I was not really enthusiastic of building new "products" with software. But then, one day, in december 2023, I discovered Julia Evan's [blog]. There was something magical about her posts. I was just browsing the home page and found post titles like: "How much memory is my process using?", and "I think I found a Mac kernel bug?". I thought to myself: What? Someone is having fun with memory? With kernel bugs? What are those things? And then you read some of her posts and you can *feel* her joy in doing the nerdy and cool stuff. I wanted that to myself. I wanted to have fun with computers. 

Not long after that, I discovered Jamie Brandon's [reflections on coding]. That dude was playing around with databases, shell, etc, etc, etc. I wanted to be like him. Like Julia. I didn´t want to learn "new tech" everyday. I wanted to have fun with the things that don't change. To be a hacker in the sense: exploring, being curious, building things that aren´t useful just for the sake of it.

I really wish I had made contact with these possibilities in the beginning of my career. And, if you are just starting, think about it. If you love computers, like I do, just try something fun for the sake of it.
